Foundation Models for Visual Place Recognition in Mid-Sized Anatolian Cities: 
A Systematic Evaluation of DINOv2-Based Pipelines on Kırşehir 
 
 
 
Abstract 
Visual place recognition (VPR) has advanced rapidly with the rise of self -supervised 
foundation models, yet benchmarks remain geographically concentrated on large 
Western and East Asian cities. Whether the design choices established on these 
benchmarks tra nsfer to mid -sized, architecturally homogeneous cities outside this 
distribution is an open question. This study presents a systematic empirical evaluation 
of a DINOv2-based two-stage VPR pipeline on Kırşehir, a mid -sized Anatolian city in 
Central Turkey, and examines how its main design choices — partial fine -tuning, 
pooling strategy, local -feature re -ranking, and backbone size — behave under this 
visual regime. We constructed a Google Street View dataset of 26,220 images 
captured at 6,555 unique coordinate points on 654 streets with four cardinal headings 
per point, covering approximately 28.80 km². The dataset was partitioned via a 0.002°-
resolution grid-based geographic split into 18,140 training, 6,060 database, and 2,020 
query images. The pipeline combines a partially fine-tuned DINOv2 ViT-B/14 backbone 
(last four Transformer blocks unfrozen) with Generalized Mean (GeM) pooling and a 
512-dimensional projection head, trained under Multi -Similarity Loss with hard 
negative mining, followed by an ALIKED + LightGlue local-matching re-ranking stage. 
Controlled ablations isolated the contributions of the loss function, the pooling strategy, 
the backbone size, and two re -ranking criteria. Partial fine -tuning improved Top -1 
street-name accuracy from 56.14% (froze n DINOv2) to 74.70% and reduced the 
median localization error from 49.8 m to 8.2 m. Contrary to the common assumption in 
the VPR literature, a CLS -token variant slightly outperformed the GeM -pooled main 
model (76.58% vs. 74.70% street accuracy; 5.3 m vs. 8.2 m median error). LightGlue-
based re-ranking degraded the output of the global descriptor in every configuration 
tested — uniform application dropped street accuracy to 48.32–49.41%, and a 
conditional pipeline produced a monotonic performance decline across six confidence 
thresholds. The ViT-S/14 variant retained 94.5% of the R@1<100 m performance of 
ViT-B/14 while using 25.6% of the parameters and 34.3% of the single-image inference 
time on an NVIDIA L4 GPU. A spatial error analysis attributed 16.2% of errors to high-
confidence perceptual aliasing concentrated on repetitive residential streets and the 
long-tail errors to database -sparsity effects. Conclusions. Foundation-model-based 
VPR delivers strong single -city performance on this under -represented urban setting 
when the last few Transformer blocks are fine -tuned, but several design choices 
considered optimal on large Western benchmarks — GeM pooling over CLS 
aggregation and local-feature re-ranking as a default second stage — do not transfer. 
A single-stage ViT-S/14 pipeline offers a favorable accuracy –efficiency trade-off for 
deployment-oriented systems. The reported negative result on LightGlue re -ranking 
highlights the need for city -specific empirical validation of standard VPR design 
patterns. 
Keywords: Visual place recognition; foundation models; DINOv2; fine -tuning; GeM 
pooling; LightGlue; re -ranking; vision transformer; Google Street View; computer 
vision, machine learning. 
 
Introduction 
Visual place recognition (VPR) is the task of estimating where an image was taken by 
matching it against a geo -referenced database (Lowry et al. 2015) . It is a core 
component of mobile robotics, autonomous driving, augmented reality, and geo -
referenced media indexing, and it is one of the few localization modalities that can 
function in GPS-denied environments such as de nse urban canyons, indoor spaces, 
and tunnels. The last decade has seen a rapid evolution of VPR methods, from bag -
of-words and VLAD aggregation of hand -crafted descriptors (Cummins & Newman 
2008), through learned CNN-based global descriptors such as NetVLAD (Arandjelovic 
et al. 2016), to large-scale classification-based training schemes (Berton et al. 2022)  
and, most recently, the adoption of foundation models as backbones (Oquab et al. 
2023). 
Despite this progress, VPR benchmarks have remained geographically concentrated. 
The widely used Pittsburgh30k, Tokyo24/7, and Mapillary Street -Level Sequences 
(MSLS) datasets cover predominantly North American, Japanese, and globally 
distributed but photographer-curated urban imagery (Arandjelovic et al. 2016; Torii et 
al. 2015; Warburg et al. 2020) , and the main large -scale training datasets — San 
Francisco eXtra Large (SF -XL) and GSV -Cities — center on North American and 
major-city imagery (Berton et al. 2022). Studies that validate VPR methods on mid -
sized, non-Western, architecturally homogeneous cities remain scarce, which has two 
related consequences. First, it is difficult to assess how well the design choices 
established on these benchmarks genera lize to the urban distribution in which many 
practical deployments would occur — Anatolian, Balkan, Middle Eastern, or Central 
Asian cities that share a distinctive built -environment profile. Second, negative or 
contradictory results on such cities are largely invisible, which can produce an inflated 
sense of how well standard two-stage pipelines transfer across contexts. 
This paper contributes a systematic empirical study of foundation-model-based visual 
place recognition on Kırşehir, a mid -sized Anatolian city in Central Turkey, with three 
characteristic properties that make it a useful stress test for standard VPR 
methodology: (i) a compact urban core of approximately 28.80 km² with limited 
topographic variation, (ii) a building stock dominated by repetit ive low -to-mid-rise 
residential facades, sharing a narrow stylistic vocabulary, and (iii) dense narrow-street 
geometry in which cross -street visual differences are fine -grained rather than 
categorical. These properties amplify the fine -grained retrieval pr oblem and make 
Kırşehir a challenging test bed for a class of methods that were designed and validated 
under considerably different visual statistics. 
Motivated by the observations above, the present study is organized around four 
research questions. 
RQ1 — Adaptation. How much of the performance of a DINOv2 -based VPR pipeline 
on a mid-sized, architecturally homogeneous Anatolian city is contributed by the pre -
trained foundation model itself, and how much by fine-tuning on a city-specific dataset? 
Specifically, what is the gap between a frozen DINOv2 feature extractor and a partially 
fine-tuned one, and is the gap uniform across recall thresholds? 
RQ2 — Design Choices. On such a dataset, do the default design choices of 
foundation-model-based VPR — patch-level GeM pooling and Multi-Similarity Loss — 
dominate alternatives in a controlled ablation? In particular, how does CLS -token 
aggregation compare with GeM pooling when both are trained under identical 
conditions? 
RQ3 — Re-Ranking. Does a standard LightGlue-based re-ranking stage improve upon 
a well-adapted global descriptor on Google Street View imagery of a small Anatolian 
city, and if not, what are the structural reasons for the discrepancy with results reported 
on large urban benchmarks? 
RQ4 — Efficiency. What is the accuracy–efficiency trade-off between the ViT-S/14 and 
ViT-B/14 variants of DINOv2 under the identical fine -tuning protocol, and does the 
smaller backbone preserve the dominant patterns observed with the larger one? 
This paper makes the following contributions. 
(i) We construct and analyze a dedicated Google Street View dataset of Kırşehir 
comprising 26,220 images captured at 6,555 unique coordinate points across 654 
streets, with four cardinal headings per point and an approximately 28.80 km² coverage 
area. The dataset is partitioned into training, database, and query sets using a grid -
based geographic split that guards against spatial leakage between train and test. 
(ii) We present a systematic evaluation of a two-stage DINOv2-based VPR pipeline on 
this dataset, including ablations over the loss function (Triplet vs. Multi -Similarity), the 
pooling strategy (patch-level GeM vs. CLS token), the backbone size (ViT-S/14 vs. ViT-
B/14), and two criteria for LightGlue-based re-ranking (match count vs. a hybrid score). 
(iii) We report and analyze a concrete negative result: on this dataset, LightGlue-based 
re-ranking consistently degrades the output of the fine-tuned global descriptor, whether 
applied uniformly or selectively to low-confidence queries. We identify three structural 
causes grounded in the properties of the imagery and the pipeline, and we position this 
finding as a domain-specific limitation of an otherwise well-established design pattern. 
(iv) We show that the ViT -S/14 variant of DINOv2 re tains 94.5% of the R@1<100 m 
performance of ViT-B/14 while using only 25.6% of the parameters and 34.3% of the 
single-image inference time, and we argue that this configuration is a defensible default 
for embedded and mobile deployment scenarios. 
(v) We provide a detailed spatial and perceptual aliasing analysis that disentangles two 
distinct error regimes — high-confidence aliasing on repetitive residential streets, and 
long-tail errors driven by database sparsity — and we quantify their relative 
contributions to overall error. 
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the dataset 
and the train/database/query split protocol. Section 3 details the two -stage 
methodology, including the DINOv2 backbone, the fine -tuning procedure, the GeM-
pooled projection head, the Multi-Similarity Loss training, and the ALIKED + LightGlue 
re-ranking stage. Section 4 presents the experimental results, including the main 
comparison, the conditional re -ranking pipeline, the ViT -S vs. ViT-B comparison, the 
confidence-rejection analysis, the computational cost, and the spatial and perceptual 
aliasing analyses. Section 5 discusses the findings in the context of existing literature 
and analyzes their probable causes. Section 6 concludes the paper with a summary of 
contributions and directions for future work. 
 
 
Literature Review 
Visual place recognition and its close relative visual geo -localization have been 
surveyed comprehensively  in (Lowry et al. 2015) . The field is typically organized 
around two design axes: the representation used to describe each place, and the 
matching strategy used to compare a query against the database. We briefly review 
progress along each axis, with an emphasis on developments relevant to the present 
study. 
Early appearance-based approaches represented each place by a bag-of-words vector 
over hand-crafted local descriptors such as SIFT or SURF, with perceptual aliasing 
handled probabilistically (Cummins & Newman 2008) . The introduction of learnable 
global descriptors, most notably NetVLAD (Arandjelovic et al. 2016), enabled end-to-
end training from weakly supervised Street View tuples and produced substantial 
accuracy gains across standard benchmarks. Subsequent developments along this 
line include patch-level extensions that retain local spa tial information (Hausler et al. 
2021) (Patch-NetVLAD), feature-mixing aggregators that replace VLAD with all -MLP 
modules (Ali-Bey et al. 2023)  (MixVPR), and large-scale classification-based training 
that avoids expensive mining  (Berton et al. 2022)   (CosPlace). Generalized Mean 
(GeM) pooling (Radenović et al. 2018) provides a learnable trade-off between average 
and max pooling over patch features and has become the dominant pooling choice for 
transformer-based VPR models. 
Local feature matching has followed a parallel trajectory, from hand -crafted detectors 
and descriptors to learned keypoint–descriptor combinations such as ALIKED (Zhao et 
al. 2023) and graph-neural-network-based matchers such as LightGlue (Lindenberger 
et al. 2023) . In two -stage VPR, a global descriptor is used to retrieve the Top -N 
candidates and a local matcher is then applied to re -rank them via geometric 
verification (Hausler et al. 2021). This global-then-local pipeline has become a de facto 
standard on benchmarks that exhibit strong viewpoint or appearance cha nge (Sattler 
et al. 2018; Warburg et al. 2020) , under the assumption that local matching can filter 
out candidates that are globally similar but geometrically inconsistent. 
The most recent development relevant to this study is the emergence of visual 
foundation models — large self-supervised vision models pre -trained on broad data 
and adapted to a range of downstream tasks (Bommasani et al. 2021) . The Vision 
Transformer architecture (Dosovitskiy et al. 2020)  provides the backbone for most of 
these models, and DINOv2 (Oquab et al. 2023)  has recently been established as a 
particularly strong self-supervised vision backbone, delivering competitive or state-of-
the-art results across classification, segmentation, depth estimation, and retrieval. A 
growing body of work shows that adapting DINOv2 to VPR via partial fine -tuning, 
adapters, or lightweight projection heads yields strong performance on standard 
benchmarks (Berton et al. 2022; Oquab et  al. 2023). However, the question of how 
such adaptation behaves on small, architecturally homogeneous, non -Western cities 
— the setting of interest here — has received comparatively little attention. 
Alongside accuracy, practical VPR deployments are co nstrained by compute and 
memory (Lowry et al. 2015), and a large body of work on parameter-efficient adaptation 
has emerged to address these constraints, including Low -Rank Adaptation (Hu et al. 
2022) and lightweight adapter modules. The trade-off between backbone capacity and 
deployment feasibility — studied here through a direct comparison of the ViT-S/14 and 
ViT-B/14 variants of DINOv2 — is therefore relevant both for production systems and 
for reducing the computational footprint of ongoing research. A parallel concern is the 
calibration of retrieval confidence, which remains poorly understood for deep models 
(Guo et al. 2017) and has direct implications for safety-critical deployment. 
 
 
Method 
3.1. System Overview 
This study proposes a two-stage Visual Place Recognition (VPR) system for the city center of Kırşehir, 
Türkiye. The first stage is a retrieval module that extrac ts a global descriptor from a query image and 
performs vector search to obtain candidate locations. In this stage, the self-supervised vision foundation 
model DINOv2 (Oquab et al. 2023)  is used as the backbone and is fine -tuned under a metric learning 
framework to capture the visual characteristics specific to Kırşehir. The second stage is designed to re-
rank the Top -N candidates returned by the first stage using the local feature matcher LightGlue 
(Lindenberger et al. 2023). An overview of the proposed pipeline is shown in Figure 1.

extraction followed by F AISS-based Top-K retrieval. Stage 2: Local feature matching-based re-ranking with 
LightGlue. 
3.2. Dataset 
3.2.1. Data Collection 
The dataset used in this study was constructed from the city center of Kırşehir using the Google Street 
View API. During acquisition, the street network was sampled at approximately 50-meter intervals, and 
at each sampled point four images were obtained corresponding to the four cardinal headings (North, 
0°; East, 90°; South, 180°; West, 270°). Each image filename was structured to encode the geographic 
coordinates (latitude and longitude) and the heading angle, yielding a directly retrievable location label 
for every image. The resulting dataset covers 6,555 unique geographic points distributed across 654 
distinct streets/avenues. The image resolution is fixed at 640 × 640 pixels. The spatial cove rage is 
approximately 28.80 km² (6.10 km north–south and 4.72 km east–west). The geographic distribution of 
the dataset is shown in Figure 2. 

 
Figure 2. Geographic distribution of the Kırşehir VPR dataset. The left panel illustrates the grid-based train/test 
split (train in blue, test in red); the right panel shows the per-street image density across the city. 
3.2.2. Train/Test Split 
To eliminate the data leakage risk commonly encountered in VPR studies, images were partitioned into 
training and test sets using a geographic block-based (grid-based) scheme rather than random sampling. 
Each image was assigned to a grid cell of 0.002° side length (approximately 222 m). Of the 444 resulting 
grid blocks, 70% (310 blocks) were assigned to training and 30% (134 bl ocks) to testing. To ensure 
reproducibility, the assignment was performed with a fixed random seed (seed = 42). This strategy 
guarantees that the model is evaluated on geographic regions it has never seen during training. 
Within the test set, a realistic VPR scenario was emulated by separating, at each coordinate point, the 
last recorded heading as the query and the remaining headings as database (DB) entries. The model is 
therefore required to retrieve a single -heading query against DB images taken at dif ferent headings of 
the same location. Points with fewer than two images were excluded from evaluation. After these 
operations, the final dataset consists of 18,140 training, 6,060 DB, and 2,020 query images. A summary 
of dataset statistics is provided in Table 1.

3.3. DINOv2-Based Global Descriptor (Stage 1) 
3.3.1. Backbone Model 
Global visual descriptors in the first stage are extracted with DINOv2 [1], a self -supervised vision 
foundation model pre-trained on large-scale curated image datasets, which is known to produce features 
with strong transferability. The main model uses the  ViT-B/14 variant of DINOv2 (ViT-Base, 14 × 14 
patch size). In addition, as part of the size –performance trade-off analysis, the ViT-S/14 variant (ViT-
Small, 14 × 14) is trained under the same framework for comparison (see Section 3.6.3). 
3.3.2. Feature Pooling and Projection Head 
DINOv2 provides two standard representations: spatial patch tokens and a global classification (CLS) 
token. Because spatial cues such as building facades, window alignments, and street perspective are 
informative for VPR, the main model adopts Generalized Mean (GeM) pooling over the patch tokens 
(Radenović et al. 2018) to preserve this spatial information. The GeM pooling operation is defined in 
Equation (1): 
f_GeM = ( (1/N) Σᵢ xᵢᵖ )^(1/p)          (1) 
Here xᵢ denotes the activations of the patch tokens, N the number of patches, and p ≥ 1 a learnable 
exponent. In this study p is initialized at 3 and updated jointly with the other network parameters during 
training. 
Following GeM pooling, the resulting embedding is mapped through a projection head to the final 512-
dimensional descriptor. The projection head is a two-layer block consisting of Linear → BatchNorm → 
ReLU → Linear. An L2-normalization is applied at the output, producing a descriptor directly usable 
with cosine similarity-based search. 
 
Figure 3. Block diagram of the proposed VPR-DINOv2 architecture. The last four Transformer blocks of the 
DINOv2 backbone are unfrozen; patch tokens are aggregated via GeM pooling and passed through a two-layer 
projection head to yield a 512-dimensional L2-normalized descriptor . 
3.3.3. Partial Fine-Tuning 
Fine-tuning all parameters of foundation models on a small dataset may lead to overfitting. To mitigate 
this risk, a partial fine-tuning strategy is employed. Out of the 12 Transformer blocks in DINOv2 ViT-
B/14, only the last four (blocks.8, 9, 10, 11) alo ng with the final normalization layer are unfrozen; all 
remaining backbone parameters are kept frozen. The projection head is initialized from scratch and 
trained end-to-end. This strategy preserves the general visual representational power of the backbone  
while allowing the final layers to adapt to the local architectural characteristics of Kırşehir. In the main 
model, 29,369,601 out of 87,566,337 total parameters (33.5%) are left trainable. 

3.4. Metric Learning-Based Fine-Tuning 
3.4.1. Retrieval Formulation 
VPR is fundamentally a retrieval problem; thus, a metric learning formulation is adopted rather than 
standard multi-class classification. The objective is to learn a discriminative embedding space in which 
images from the same location are placed close together and images from different locations are placed 
apart. In the training set, each unique coordinate point (point_id) is treated as a class, and labels are 
transformed into integers via LabelEncoder. Under this formulation, every class in the trainin g set 
contains exactly four examples (N/E/S/W headings). 
3.4.2. MPerClassSampler 
To enable heading -aware learning, MPerClassSampler (Musgrave et al. 2020)  is used instead of a 
standard random sampler. This sampler guarantees that each training batch contains exactly m samples 
from the same class (same coordinate point). In this study, m = 4 is chosen so that the four cardinal 
headings of a given coordinate are forced into the same batch. This allows the metric learning loss to 
directly assess the relationships between different headings of the same location. 
3.4.3. Multi-Similarity Loss and Hard Negative Mining 
The Multi-Similarity (MS) Loss (Wang et al. 2019) is used as the training objective. Unlike the classical 
Triplet loss, MS Loss evaluates all pairs within a batch using similarity-aware weighting and emphasizes 
the hardest positive and negative pairs. This property is crucial for the model to distinguish visually 
similar but geographically distinct streets (the perceptual aliasing problem). The MS Loss parameters 
are set to α = 2, β = 50, and base = 0.5; hard pair mining is performed with a MultiSimilarityMiner 
whose ε parameter is set to 0.1. 
3.4.4. Optimization and Training Protocol 
Training is performed with differential learning rates: trainable parameters within the DINOv2 backbone 
are updated with a learning rate of 1 × 10⁻⁵, whereas the randomly initialized projection head is updated 
with 1 × 10⁻⁴. This choice aims to prevent catastrophic forgetting of the pre -trained representations in 
the backbone. The Adam optimizer is used with a batch size of 32 and a total of 5 epochs. Input images 
are resized to 518 × 518 pixels (a multiple of DINOv2's patch size of 14) and normalized using the 
standard ImageNet mean and standard deviation. Data augmentation is restricted to color -based 
ColorJitter (brightness = 0.3, contrast = 0.3, saturation = 0.3); geometric augmentations are deliberately 
avoided because they would distort the geographic consistency critical to VPR. All hyperparameters are 
summarized in Table 2.

3.5. Vector Database and Global Retrieval 
After fine -tuning, the model is switched to inference mode and used to generate 512 -dimensional 
embeddings for all images in the DB and query sets. DB embeddings are L2 -normalized and indexed 
using the IndexFlatIP structure from the Facebook AI Similarity Search (FAISS) library (Douze et al. 
2025). Because L2-normalized vectors yield an inner product equal to the cosine similarity, this indexing 
choice enables cosine-based search with constant-cost inner-product computation. For each query, the 
K nearest candidates are retrieved with a single dot-product search. 
3.6. Comparative Experimental Framework 
To isolate the contribution of the main model and quantify the effect of individual architectural choices, 
additional experiments are organized under four themes. All of  these experiments use the same data 
split, the same random seed, and the same evaluation set as the main model. 
3.6.1. Baseline: Frozen DINOv2 
To quantify the contribution of fine-tuning, a baseline model is constructed in which all parameters of 
the DINO v2 backbone are frozen and only the projection head is trained. This baseline uses GeM 
pooling, MS Loss, and identical training conditions to the main model. It therefore measures the 
performance attainable with off -the-shelf DINOv2 representations without  any adaptation to the 
Kırşehir dataset. 
3.6.2. Ablation Studies 
Two core design decisions of the main model — the pooling strategy and the loss function — are 
examined through ablations. 
CLS token vs. GeM pooling: A variant is trained in which the CLS token of DINOv2 is used directly 
in place of GeM pooling. The GeM module is disabled while all other components (projection head, 
loss, optimizer) are held identical to the main model, thereby isolating the effect of spatial patch 
information against a global CLS summary for the VPR task. 
Triplet Loss vs. Multi -Similarity Loss: A variant is trained in which MS Loss is replaced by the 
classical Triplet Margin Loss (Schroff et al. 2015) . A Triplet Margin Miner (margin = 0.2, 
type_of_triplets = "hard") is used for hard triplet mining; all other components are held identical to the 
main model. 
3.6.3. DINOv2 V ariant Comparison: ViT-S/14 vs. ViT-B/14 
To examine the size–performance trade-off, which is of practical relevance for deployment, the DINOv2 
ViT-S/14 variant (ViT-Small, 384-dimensional backbone embedding, ≈ 22M parameters) is fine-tuned 
from scratch using the same protocol. In this model the last four blocks of the backbone are unfrozen, 
and the projection head maps the 384 -dimensional backbone output to the shared 512 -dimensional 
representation space. The size–speed–accuracy comparison additionally reports single-image inference 
time and on-disk model size. 
3.6.4. Confidence-Based Rejection 
The ability of a system to abstai n when uncertain is valuable in practical deployments. To this end, a 
rejection mechanism based on the DINOv2 Top-1 cosine similarity score is introduced. A threshold θ is 
swept over [0.60, 0.95] with steps of 0.01; for each θ, (i) the coverage (fraction of accepted queries), (ii) 
the localization error computed only over accepted queries, and (iii) the Recall@K metrics are reported. 
This analysis also quantifies whether the cosine similarity score is a suitable signal for confidence 
calibration. 
3.7. LightGlue-Based Local Feature Re-Ranking (Stage 2) 
3.7.1. Local Feature Extraction and Matching 
The second stage re -ranks the Top -N candidates from the first stage using local feature matching. 
ALIKED (Zhao et al. 2023) is used as the local keypoint extractor, and LightGlue (Lindenberger et al. 
2023) is used as the matcher. ALIKED is an efficient learned keypoint extractor based on deformable 
convolutions; LightGlue is a graph neural network-based matcher that incorporates an adaptive stopping 
mechanism for runtime efficiency. For each image, at most 1,024 keypoints are extracted. 
3.7.2. Ranking Criteria 
To assess whether the number of matches alone is a sufficient ranking criterion at the output of 
LightGlue, two criteria are compared: 
Match Count: Ranking based solely on the number of mutually matched keypoints. 
Hybrid Score: A score defined by the product of the match count and the mean match confidence 
(matching score): score = num_matches × (1 + avg_confidence). This formulation reduces the advantage 
gained by candidates with many but low-confidence matches. 
3.7.3. Conditional Two-Stage Pipeline 
Local feature re-ranking carries a high computational cost. Applying LightGlue to all Top-N candidates 
for every query is undesirable in practical deployment scenarios. Therefore, a conditional (adaptive) 
two-stage pipeline is proposed: if the DINOv2 Top-1 cosine similarity score is greater than or equal to 
a threshold θ, the DINOv2 Top-1 prediction is accepted directly; otherwise, the Top -20 candidates are 
re-ranked with LightGlue and the new Top -1 is returned. Six threshold values (0.70, 0.75, 0.78, 0.80, 
0.82, 0.85) are tested in the range [0.70, 0.85]. A reference condition in which LightGlue is applied 
uniformly to all queries (no threshold) is also reported for comparison. 
3.8. Evaluation Metrics 
System performance is reported in four groups of metrics: 
Street-name accuracy: The fraction of queries whose Top -1 result matches the ground -truth street 
label. It is used as a semantic, label-level measure of correctness. 
GPS localization error: The Haversine great-circle distance between the query coordinate and the Top-
1 prediction coordinate, as defined in Equation (2). Both the median and the mean are reported. 
d = 2R · arcsin( √( sin²(Δφ/2) + cos φ₁ · cos φ₂ · sin²(Δλ/2) ) )          (2) 
Here R is the Earth's radius (6,371,000 m), φ and λ denote latitude and longitude respectively, and Δφ 
and Δλ denote their differences between query and prediction. 
Threshold-based recall (Recall@1 at threshold): The fraction of queries whose Top-1 prediction error 
falls below a set of standard thresholds (25 m, 50 m, 100 m, 500 m). This is the standard evaluation 
metric in VPR literature. 
Inference time: As part of the computational cost analysis, single-image DINOv2 embedding time, per-
query FAISS search time, and LightGlue re-ranking time (for 20 candidates) are measured separately. 
3.9. Experimental Environment 
All experiments are conducted in Google Colab on an NVIDIA L4 GPU (23.7 GB VRAM). The software 
stack consists of PyTorch (DINOv2 is loaded via torch.hub), pytorch-metric-learning (Multi-Similarity 
and Triplet losses, MPerClassSampler), FAISS -CPU (vector search), the official LightGlue and 
ALIKED impl ementations, scikit -learn (LabelEncoder), and folium (geographic visualization). For 
reproducibility, the random seeds of Python, NumPy, and PyTorch are fixed at 42. 
 
 
Result 
4.1. Overview 
This section reports the experimental outcomes obtained on the Kırşehir VPR 
dataset, which comprises 18,140 training, 6,060 database, and 2,020 query images. 
Results are organized in five subsections. Section 4.2 presents the main comparison 
among the frozen baseline, the two ablation variants, the proposed main model, and 
two LightGlue re-ranking variants. Section 4.3 reports the conditional re-ranking 
pipeline. Section 4.4 compares the ViT-S/14 and ViT-B/14 variants of DINOv2 under 
the size–performance trade-off. Section 4.5 presents the confidence-based rejection 
analysis. Section 4.6 reports the computational cost of each stage, and Section 4.7 
provides the error and perceptual aliasing analyses. 
4.2. Main Comparison and Ablation Results 
Table 3 summarizes the performance of all evaluated methods on the identical query 
set. Several patterns emerge from these results. First, fine-tuning a subset of DINOv2 
parameters on the Kırşehir dataset yields a substantial improvement over the frozen 
baseline. The main model reaches 74.70% street-name accuracy and an 8.2 m 
median localization error, compared to 56.14% and 49.8 m obtained by the frozen 
variant. The relative improvement is particularly pronounced at tighter recall 
thresholds: R@1 <25 m rises from 39.01% to 61.73%, indicating that fine-tuning is 
essential for obtaining near-metric localization accuracy. Second, the two ablation 
variants remain competitive but exhibit distinct trade-offs. Replacing MS Loss with the 
classical Triplet Loss degrades performance across all metrics (median error 
increases from 8.2 m to 12.3 m, R@1 <100 m decreases from 81.24% to 78.02%), 
which is consistent with the known advantage of multi-pair weighting over single-
triplet supervision in fine-grained retrieval (Wang et al., 2019). Third, and more 
unexpectedly, the CLS-token variant slightly outperforms the GeM-pooling main 
model (76.58% vs. 74.70% street accuracy; 5.3 m vs. 8.2 m median error). This 
result deviates from the prevailing assumption in the VPR literature that spatially 
pooled patch tokens carry more discriminative information than global class tokens, 
and is further discussed in Section 5. 
In contrast, the LightGlue re-ranking stage does not improve upon the Stage-1 output 
when applied uniformly to all queries. Both the match-count and hybrid-score ranking 
criteria lead to a pronounced drop in performance: street-name accuracy falls from 
74.70% to 48.32–49.41%, median error grows from 8.2 m to 60.4–64.8 m, and R@1 
<100 m drops from 81.24% to 56.78–58.17%. This outcome, although contrary to the 
typical global-then-local VPR design (Hausler et al. 2021), is consistent across both 
ranking criteria and is stable throughout the full test set of 2,020 queries. A more 
detailed analysis of this phenomenon is presented through the conditional pipeline 
results in Section 4.3.

low-confidence queries. In other words, the local geometric cues captured by ALIKED

representation produced by the fine-tuned DINOv2 model. Potential causes for this 
behavior are discussed in Section 5.

4.5. Confidence-Based Rejection 
The confidence-based rejection analysis sweeps the DINOv2 Top-1 cosine similarity 
threshold θ across the interval [0.60, 0.95]. The observed score distribution for the 
2,020 queries is tightly concentrated in [0.65, 0.95] (Figure 7, right), with no query 
scoring below 0.66. As a result, thresholds θ ≤ 0.60 retain 100% coverage and yield 
the baseline metrics of the main model (74.70% street accuracy, 8.2 m median error, 
81.24% R@1 <100 m). Increasing θ beyond 0.70 progressively reduces coverage; 
however, the corresponding gain in accuracy among accepted queries is negligible. 
The accuracy–coverage trade-off curve (Figure 7, left) is essentially flat across the 
entire coverage range between approximately 20% and 100%, with R@1 <100 m 
remaining within a 3–5 percentage-point band. The median error curve (Figure 7, 
middle) likewise remains near the baseline value of 8 m up to θ ≈ 0.93 and only rises 
when fewer than ∼10% of queries are accepted. Quantitatively, the automatically 
selected operating point — chosen by maximizing the harmonic mean of coverage 
and R@1 <100 m — coincides with θ = 0.60 at full coverage, indicating that no 
confidence threshold improves upon accepting all queries. 
This result suggests that the cosine similarity between the query descriptor and its 
nearest database neighbor is not a well-calibrated confidence signal for the fine-
tuned DINOv2 model on this dataset. The distribution of scores is narrow and largely 
overlaps between correct and incorrect predictions (see the score-distribution 
analysis in Section 4.7), which limits the usefulness of score-based rejection. 
Calibration-aware confidence estimation is therefore identified as a direction for 
future work.

4.7. Error and Perceptual Aliasing Analysis 
4.7.1. Error Distribution 
The distribution of Top-1 localization errors for the main model is highly skewed: of 
the 2,020 queries, 1,207 (59.8%) have errors below 25 m, an additional 204 (10.1%) 
fall between 25 and 50 m, 191 (9.5%) between 50 and 100 m, 134 (6.6%) between 
100 and 250 m, 71 (3.5%) between 250 and 500 m, and 174 (8.6%) exceed 500 m. 
The median error (8.2 m) therefore lies well below the mean (179.6 m), indicating a 
small but heavy-tailed subset of queries that are mislocalized by hundreds of meters. 
Among the 2,020 queries, 1,509 (74.7%) receive the correct street label as the Top-1 
result and 511 (25.3%) do not. Figure 8 shows the error histogram together with the 
spatial distribution of correct and incorrect predictions across the city.

4.7.2. Per-Street Patterns 
The per-street error analysis reveals a clear relationship between database density 
and localization accuracy. Streets with the lowest median errors (≤ 10 m) are those 
with high database coverage, such as 1090. Sokak (n = 30, 87% street-correct, 
median 8 m), Mustafa Kemal Paşa Caddesi (n = 9, 100% correct, 8 m), and Şehit 
Sahir Kurutluoğlu Caddesi (n = 24, 88% correct, 9 m). Conversely, the largest errors 
appear on streets represented by only one or a few query points — for example 
1731. Sokak (n = 1, 3,982 m), 1396. Sokak (n = 1, 3,392 m), and Şehit Turgut 
İlanbey Caddesi (n = 1, 2,640 m). This pattern indicates that the model's performance 
is substantially more reliable on streets with denser database representation, 
whereas isolated, sparsely sampled points in the test blocks are at risk of being 
matched to visually similar streets far away. 
A scatter plot of retrieval confidence versus error (Figure 9, left) further reveals that 
high cosine similarity scores do not guarantee small localization errors: a 
considerable fraction of queries with scores in the 0.85–0.95 range are nevertheless 
mislocalized by hundreds of meters. This observation motivates the perceptual 
aliasing analysis in the next subsection.

4.7.3. Perceptual Aliasing 
Perceptual aliasing is operationalized here as cases in which a query receives a Top-
1 cosine similarity score in the upper quartile (> 0.877) but is nevertheless assigned 
to an incorrect street. Among the 511 incorrect predictions produced by the main 
model, 83 (16.2%) fall into this high-confidence subset. These high-confidence errors 
exhibit a median localization error of 59 m, substantially lower than the 258 m median 
of the low-confidence errors. In other words, perceptual aliasing produces a small but 
reliably near-miss category of confident misidentifications rather than gross 
mislocalizations. 
The streets most affected by perceptual aliasing are predominantly narrow residential 
streets with repetitive building facades: 417. Sokak (3/4 queries, 75%), 452. Sokak 
(3/8, 38%), İkizarası Caddesi (3/8, 38%), 418. Sokak (3/9, 33%) and 720. Sokak 
(4/15, 27%). The most frequent confusion pairs (e.g., 603. Sokak → 606. Sokak, 283. 
Sokak → 282. Sokak, 418./417. Sokak → 452. Sokak) involve adjacent or nearby 
streets within the same residential block, which supports the interpretation that 
perceptual aliasing is primarily driven by locally homogeneous architectural styles 
rather than distant coincidences. Retrieval diversity analysis corroborates this view: 
within the Top-10 results, the median number of distinct streets is 3, 12.0% of queries 
return a single consistent street, and 27.0% return five or more different streets, 
reflecting the proportion of queries for which the model is unable to narrow down to a 
single place confidently. 
Finally, the score distribution of correct versus incorrect predictions overlaps 
substantially across the range [0.75, 0.95] (Figure 10, left). Although the incorrect-
prediction distribution is slightly shifted toward lower scores, the two distributions are 
not separable by a single threshold, which is the underlying reason that confidence-
based rejection (Section 4.5) produces no accuracy gains.

Discussion 
5.1. Summary of Key Findings 
The experimental evidence presented in Section 4 yields four principal findings that 
collectively shape the discussion of this study. First, partial fine-tuning of DINOv2 is 
highly effective: the main model achieves 81.24% Recall@1 at 100 m and an 8.2 m 
median localization error, improving upon the frozen baseline by 17.13 percentage 
points at the 100 m threshold and reducing the median error by a factor of six. 
Second, two architectural choices commonly considered critical for retrieval — patch-
level GeM pooling and Multi-Similarity Loss — deliver competitive but not strictly 
dominant performance on this dataset: Multi-Similarity Loss outperforms Triplet Loss 
as expected (Wang et al. 2019), whereas, unexpectedly, the CLS-token variant 
slightly surpasses the GeM-pooled main model. Third, the two-stage pipeline with 
LightGlue-based re-ranking degrades performance in every configuration tested, 
whether applied uniformly or only to low-confidence queries. Fourth, the ViT-S/14 
variant retains 94.5% of the ViT-B/14 R@1<100 m performance while using only 
25.6% of its parameters and 34.3% of its inference time. The discussion below 
situates each of these findings within the literature and analyzes their probable 
causes and implications. 
5.2. Why Does Partial Fine-Tuning Help So Much on This Dataset? 
The large gap between the frozen DINOv2 baseline (56.14% street accuracy, 49.8 m 
median error) and the fine-tuned main model (74.70% street accuracy, 8.2 m median 
error) is considerably larger than gaps commonly reported on widely used VPR 
benchmarks such as Pittsburgh30k or Tokyo24/7 (Berton et al. 2022). We attribute 
this to the specific visual regime of the Kırşehir dataset. Kırşehir is a mid-sized 
Anatolian city dominated by repetitive low-to-mid-rise residential buildings, narrow 
side streets, and a relatively homogeneous architectural vocabulary. DINOv2 is pre-
trained on a large and diverse corpus biased toward Western, English-speaking 
contexts (Oquab et al. 2023); as a consequence, its off-the-shelf features are 

descriptive but under-specialized for the fine-grained distinctions required in a small, 
architecturally uniform city. Unfreezing the last four Transformer blocks allows the 
model to remap its high-level visual tokens toward the architectural, material, and 
vegetation cues that separate Kırşehir streets, while leaving the lower-level 
representations intact. This interpretation is consistent with the general finding that 
parameter-efficient adaptation of foundation models to domain-shifted data is 
essential for strong downstream performance (Berton et al. 2022; Oquab et al. 2023). 
5.3. CLS Token vs. GeM Pooling: An Unexpected Ablation Outcome 
The prevailing assumption in the VPR literature is that spatially pooled patch tokens 
preserve more discriminative information than a single global CLS token (Hausler et 
al. 2021; Radenović et al. 2018). Our results partially contradict this expectation: the 
CLS-token variant achieves 76.58% street accuracy and 5.3 m median error against 
74.70% and 8.2 m for the GeM-pooled main model. The gap, while small, is 
consistent across all Recall@K thresholds (Table 3), so it is unlikely to be an artefact 
of a single metric. 
Several plausible factors explain this pattern. First, DINOv2 is explicitly trained with a 
discriminative self-supervised objective (DINO-style self-distillation combined with 
iBOT masked-image modeling) that produces a CLS token already well-positioned in 
the representational space (Oquab et al. 2023); in other transformer encoders without 
such training, the CLS token is often weaker than pooled patch features, but in 
DINOv2 it is a strong global descriptor in its own right. Second, the Kırşehir dataset 
exhibits limited intra-street spatial variation: within a single street segment at four 
cardinal headings, the patch-level statistics change substantially (e.g., one facade 
per image), while the semantic content (residential apartment blocks of similar style) 
remains consistent. A GeM-pooled representation may therefore absorb more 
heading-specific variance than is useful for cross-heading matching, whereas the 
CLS token provides a more heading-invariant summary. Third, our training schedule 
is short (5 epochs) and the patch-token GeM exponent is a learnable parameter; 
longer training may allow GeM to surpass CLS, but we did not observe clear signs of 
underfitting in the training loss, which plateaued after the third epoch. These 
observations suggest that the default choice of GeM pooling for DINOv2-based VPR 
should not be assumed to be strictly optimal, particularly on small, architecturally 
homogeneous cities; ablations against CLS pooling deserve routine inclusion in 
future studies. 
5.4. Why Does LightGlue-Based Re-Ranking Degrade Performance? 
The most striking negative result in this study is that LightGlue-based re-ranking 
harms performance in all configurations tested. Uniform re-ranking across the Top-20 
candidates reduces street accuracy from 74.70% to 48.32–49.41%, and the 
conditional pipeline produces a monotonic degradation as the fraction of LightGlue-
processed queries grows (Table 4). This outcome contrasts sharply with the well-
documented advantages of two-stage global-then-local pipelines on standard VPR 
benchmarks (Hausler et al. 2021; Lindenberger et al. 2023), and therefore requires 
careful examination. 
We identify three likely, non-mutually-exclusive causes. The first concerns the 
imagery itself. Google Street View images are produced by a multi-camera rig and 
are stitched from multiple physical sensors (Anguelov et al. 2010); they are 
geometrically re-projected and subjected to automatic white-balance and exposure 
normalization that differ across capture sessions. Local keypoint detectors such as 
ALIKED (Zhao et al. 2023) are sensitive to consistent local texture, and stitching 
seams, rolling-shutter artefacts, and automatic post-processing can introduce 
spurious keypoints. Once such keypoints dominate an image, they tend to match 
among themselves and produce high match counts that do not reflect the actual 
scene geometry. 
The second concerns the content of Kırşehir street scenes. A large fraction of the 
dataset shows plastered apartment facades, uniform sidewalks, standardized road 
surfaces, commercial signage, and repetitive window grids. These structures are, by 
construction, textures that are locally self-similar, which is exactly the regime in which 
local keypoint matchers are known to produce confident but wrong matches (Sattler 
et al. 2018). In this regime, the number of matches between a query and a non-
matching candidate (e.g., a similarly styled apartment on a different street) can 
exceed the number of matches to the correct place if the correct place happens to be 
viewed from a different heading and therefore shares fewer directly comparable local 
features. 
The third concerns the structure of the Top-N pool. On large benchmarks, Stage-1 
retrieval typically returns several visually distinct candidates in its Top-N, among 
which the correct one can be isolated by geometric verification. On Kırşehir, the first-
stage output is already tightly tuned to the target (81.24% R@1 at 100 m), and the 
remaining 18.76% of Top-1 errors involve candidates that are either visually 
indistinguishable from the query (perceptual aliasing cases; Section 4.7.3) or located 
in under-sampled regions of the database. In both cases, local feature matching 
cannot recover the correct place because the information needed is not present in 
the geometry. Under these conditions, LightGlue's ranking based on match counts 
effectively re-randomizes a near-optimal ordering, which explains the consistent 
performance drop. Our hybrid-score variant — which reduces the advantage of low-
confidence matches — partially mitigates but does not reverse the effect (Table 3). 
Taken together, these observations do not imply that two-stage pipelines are 
inherently inappropriate for VPR, but rather that local geometric verification is most 
useful when Stage-1 retrieval is weaker than Stage-2 geometric reasoning. On this 
dataset, the opposite holds: the fine-tuned DINOv2 descriptor is stronger than the 
ALIKED+LightGlue evidence it could be re-ranked with. This finding aligns with 
recent work showing that for sufficiently well-adapted global descriptors, simpler 
single-stage pipelines can outperform heavier re-ranking schemes in practical 
deployments (Ali-Bey et al. 2023). 
5.5. Cosine Similarity Is Not a Usable Confidence Signal Here 
The confidence-based rejection analysis in Section 4.5 shows that sweeping the Top-
1 cosine similarity threshold across [0.60, 0.95] does not produce a coverage range 
in which rejected queries are systematically worse than accepted ones. The 
accuracy–coverage curve is nearly flat and the automatically selected operating point 
coincides with accepting all queries. The underlying reason is visible in the score 
distributions (Section 4.7.3): the DINOv2 scores of correctly and incorrectly localized 
queries overlap heavily between 0.75 and 0.95, with only a modest shift in their 
means. 
This behavior is a symptom of the broader problem of confidence calibration in deep 
retrieval models. Cosine similarity is a convenient proxy for similarity but is not a well-
calibrated posterior probability, and this discrepancy is known to persist after 
standard metric-learning fine-tuning (Guo et al., 2017). For safety-critical 
deployments — such as autonomous navigation or emergency response — a more 
principled confidence estimator is necessary. Candidate approaches include 
geometric verification applied selectively to high-risk queries, ensemble disagreement 
across multiple descriptor heads, and explicit calibration via temperature scaling or 
conformal prediction. None of these were evaluated in the present study and they 
constitute a natural next step. 
5.6. Size–Performance Trade-off: The Case for ViT-S/14 
The comparison between ViT-S/14 and ViT-B/14 (Table 5) is arguably the most 
practically important result of this study. ViT-S/14 retains 94.5% of the R@1<100 m 
performance of ViT-B/14 while using 25.6% of the parameters and 34.3% of the 
single-image inference time on identical hardware. For deployment on embedded 
systems, in-vehicle compute units, or mobile devices — all of which are the canonical 
target platforms for VPR (Lowry et al. 2015) — this trade-off is highly favorable. The 
observation is also consistent with the broader finding in representation learning that, 
for moderately sized downstream datasets, the marginal return of scaling backbone 
capacity diminishes quickly once the pre-training is strong (Oquab et al. 2023). 
From a methodological standpoint, this result also has implications for research 
practice. Many recent VPR papers adopt the largest available DINOv2 variant by 
default; our results suggest that ViT-S/14 can serve as a viable proxy during 
development and ablation studies, substantially reducing compute requirements and 
iteration time without qualitatively changing the conclusions. The dominant failure 
modes observed on ViT-S/14 (perceptual aliasing in residential streets, sparse-
sampling errors in the tail) are the same as those of ViT-B/14; the smaller model does 
not introduce new error categories, only amplifies existing ones. 
5.7. Perceptual Aliasing and Dataset Sparsity 
Two distinct error regimes emerge from the error analysis in Section 4.7. The first is 
perceptual aliasing, defined here as high-confidence (upper-quartile score) 
misclassifications. This regime accounts for 16.2% of errors, and these errors are 
geographically concentrated on narrow residential streets with repetitive facades 
(e.g., 417. Sokak with 75% aliasing rate, 452. Sokak with 38%). Such aliasing is a 
well-documented failure mode of appearance-based place recognition (Cummins & 
Newman 2008; Sattler et al. 2018), and its prevalence here reflects a real property of 
the city rather than a methodological weakness. 
The second regime, which accounts for the long right tail of the error distribution, is 
database sparsity. Streets that are represented in the test query set by a single query 
point (e.g., 1731. Sokak with n = 1 and a 3,982 m error) also have sparse database 
coverage, so no nearby candidates are available for retrieval. This is an artefact of 
the grid-based train/test split: when a geographic block falls entirely into the test side, 
the queries inside that block may have no geographically adjacent anchors in the 
database. A finer-grained split (e.g., 100 m grids instead of 222 m) or a balanced 
resampling strategy would reduce this tail, at the cost of reducing geographic 
independence between train and test. 
The operational implication is that per-query performance should not be interpreted 
as uniform across the city. For downstream applications, it would be reasonable to 
stratify reliability estimates by local database density and by the presence of 
architecturally distinctive landmarks, rather than to rely on a single global metric such 
as median error. 
5.8. Positioning Against Standard VPR Benchmarks 
A direct numerical comparison between our results and state-of-the-art scores on 
Pittsburgh30k, Tokyo24/7 or Mapillary Street-Level Sequences (MSLS) is not 
appropriate, because those benchmarks use different database sizes, different 
query–database matching conventions, and different evaluation protocols (Berton et 
al. 2022; Warburg et al. 2020). However, two qualitative observations can be safely 
made. First, our main model's 81.24% R@1 at 100 m on Kırşehir is within the range 
of performance reported by fine-tuned DINOv2 variants on Pittsburgh30k and MSLS 
for fine-tuned backbones (Berton et al. 2022; Oquab et al. 2023), suggesting that the 
fine-tuning recipe is not pathologically off. Second, the failure of LightGlue-based re-
ranking on this dataset differs from results on benchmarks where re-ranking with 
local matchers typically provides gains (Hausler et al. 2021; Lindenberger et al. 
2023), which indicates that the gap is specific to the imagery and architectural 
properties of Kırşehir rather than to the re-ranking methodology itself. Replicating 
both observations on a standard benchmark (e.g., running the same fine-tuning and 
re-ranking pipeline on MSLS or Pittsburgh30k) is an important item for future work. 
5.9. Limitations 
Several limitations of this study should be acknowledged. First, both the query and 
the database images are drawn from the same source (Google Street View), so the 
evaluation does not probe cross-source generalization. A realistic VPR deployment 
would receive queries from mobile phone cameras or dashcams, which differ from 
Street View imagery in lens distortion, resolution, exposure, and viewpoint height. 
The framework for such a cross-source evaluation is already in place in our 
demonstration pipeline, but a systematic study with a statistically meaningful number 
of mobile-source queries remains future work. 
Second, the dataset covers a single mid-sized Anatolian city. While this is precisely 
the regime that motivates the study, the conclusions about CLS vs. GeM pooling and 
about LightGlue-based re-ranking may not transfer to large, architecturally diverse 
cities. Replication on a second city with similar characteristics (e.g., a similarly sized 
Central Anatolian or Balkan city) would strengthen the generality of the domain-
specific claims. 
Third, all Street View imagery used here was captured during a single time window 
and therefore does not test seasonal, illumination, or temporal robustness — an 
aspect that long-term VPR benchmarks such as RobotCar Seasons or MSLS 
explicitly address (Maddern et al. 2017; Warburg et al. 2020). Evaluating the 
robustness of the fine-tuned model under temporal shift is a natural extension. 
Fourth, LightGlue was used with its default configuration and with only two ranking 
criteria (match count and a hybrid score). More sophisticated geometric verification, 
such as RANSAC-based inlier counting or learned re-ranking heads, might behave 
differently. Our results should therefore not be read as a general condemnation of 
local feature matching for VPR, but as a domain-specific negative result for the 
particular combination of DINOv2 global features, ALIKED keypoints, LightGlue 
matching, and Google Street View imagery on a small Anatolian city. 
Finally, the overall training protocol involves a single random seed and five epochs. 
While the random seed was fixed for reproducibility and the training loss curves were 
stable, repeated trials under multiple seeds would provide confidence intervals 
around the reported metrics. This is particularly relevant for the comparison between 
GeM and CLS pooling, where the observed gap is small. 
5.10. Implications and Directions for Future Work 
The findings of this study have three practical implications. (i) For VPR deployments 
in mid-sized, architecturally homogeneous cities outside the dominant Western urban 
distribution, foundation models such as DINOv2 require fine-tuning rather than zero-
shot use, but the fine-tuning can be kept parameter-efficient by restricting adaptation 
to the last few Transformer blocks. (ii) The default choice of pooling and re-ranking 
components in the VPR literature should not be taken as universally optimal; the CLS 
vs. GeM ablation and the negative LightGlue result illustrate that domain-specific 
evaluation of these design choices is necessary. (iii) A single-stage ViT-S/14 pipeline 
is a practical and near-lossless alternative to larger two-stage systems for this class 
of deployments. 
Five directions are particularly promising for future work. First, a systematic cross-
source evaluation using mobile-phone-originating queries would quantify the domain 
gap between Street View and consumer imagery and could be carried out with the 
demonstration pipeline already developed in this study. Second, calibration-aware 
confidence estimation — via temperature scaling, ensembles, or conformal retrieval 
sets — would address the rejection limitation documented in Section 5.5. Third, 
replicating the pipeline on standard benchmarks (Pittsburgh30k, Tokyo24/7, MSLS) 
would enable a direct comparison against the VPR state of the art and clarify which 
of the observations here generalize. Fourth, parameter-efficient adaptation 
techniques such as LoRA (Hu et al. 2022) or learned adapters could further reduce 
the number of trainable parameters and training time. Fifth, the release of the Kırşehir 
dataset — or of a script that recreates it from the Google Street View API — would 
enable replication and extension by other groups, consistent with the data-sharing 
norms of the VPR community (Berton et al. 2022). 
 
 
 
Conclusion 
This study presented a systematic empirical investigation of foundation-model-based 
visual place recognition on a mid-sized, architecturally homogeneous Anatolian city. 
We constructed a 26,220-image Google Street View dataset covering approximately 
28.80 km² of Kırşehir, organized around 6,555 unique coordinate points on 654 
streets with four cardinal headings per point, and split geographically via a 0.002°-
resolution grid to yield 18,140 training, 6,060 database, and 2,020 query images. On 
this dataset we trained a two-stage pipeline combining a partially fine-tuned DINOv2 
ViT-B/14 backbone with GeM pooling and a 512-dimensional projection head as the 
global retrieval stage, and an ALIKED + LightGlue re-ranking module as the local-
matching stage. A complementary set of ablations isolated the contributions of the 
loss function, the pooling strategy, the backbone size, and the re-ranking criterion, 
and additional analyses quantified the computational cost, the confidence-rejection 
behavior, and the spatial distribution of errors. 
6.1. Main Findings 
Four findings stand out. First, partial fine-tuning of DINOv2 is both necessary and 
highly effective in this visual regime: unfreezing the last four Transformer blocks 
raised street-name accuracy from 56.14% to 74.70% and reduced the median 
localization error from 49.8 m to 8.2 m. Second, contrary to the prevailing assumption 
in the visual place recognition literature, the CLS-token variant of our architecture 
slightly outperformed the GeM-pooled main model (76.58% vs. 74.70% street 
accuracy, 5.3 m vs. 8.2 m median error), indicating that pooling choices for DINOv2-
based pipelines should not be adopted uncritically on small, architecturally uniform 
cities. Third, LightGlue-based re-ranking degraded the output of the first stage in 
every configuration tested, whether applied uniformly (street accuracy dropping to 
48.32–49.41%) or selectively to low-confidence queries (monotonic degradation 
across all thresholds); this negative result is a concrete domain-specific limitation of 
an otherwise well-established design pattern, traceable to the stitched nature of 
Street View imagery, the repetitive textures of residential streets, and the already 
near-optimal first-stage ranking. Fourth, the ViT-S/14 variant retained 94.5% of the 
ViT-B/14 R@1<100 m performance while using 25.6% of the parameters and 34.3% 
of the inference time, establishing a practical size–performance operating point for 
deployment-oriented systems. 
6.2. Contributions 
The contributions of this work can be summarized as follows. (i) We introduced, to 
our knowledge, the first dedicated visual place recognition dataset for a mid-sized 
Anatolian city, constructed with a reproducible grid-based geographic split that 
guards against spatial leakage. (ii) We reported a systematic ablation of the main 
design choices in a DINOv2-based two-stage pipeline on this dataset, including a 
non-standard comparison between patch-level GeM pooling and CLS-token 
aggregation. (iii) We documented and analyzed a negative result — the consistent 
failure of LightGlue-based re-ranking to improve upon a well-adapted global 
descriptor on Street View imagery of a small Anatolian city — and proposed three 
specific causes grounded in the properties of the data and the pipeline. (iv) We 
showed that a single-stage ViT-S/14 configuration offers an attractive accuracy–
efficiency trade-off for this class of deployment scenarios. (v) We provided an error 
analysis that disentangles high-confidence perceptual aliasing (16.2% of errors, 
concentrated on repetitive residential streets) from long-tail database-sparsity errors 
(isolated test blocks with no nearby database anchors), highlighting that global 
metrics alone are insufficient to characterize a VPR system's reliability across a city. 
6.3. Practical Implications 
From a practical standpoint, three implications follow directly from the results. First, 
for VPR deployments outside the dominant Western urban distribution that motivates 
most existing benchmarks, foundation models such as DINOv2 cannot be used zero-
shot; a lightweight fine-tuning of the last few Transformer blocks on a city-specific 
dataset is both necessary and sufficient to obtain strong performance. Second, the 
standard global-then-local two-stage design cannot be assumed to generalize; 
practitioners should treat the Stage-2 re-ranker as a hypothesis to be tested rather 
than a default to be inherited, particularly when the imagery is stitched and the target 
city is architecturally uniform. Third, a single-stage ViT-S/14 pipeline is a defensible 
default choice for embedded and mobile deployment scenarios, where the 25.6% 
parameter footprint and 2.9× speed-up may matter more than the small residual 
performance gap. 
6.4. Outlook 
Several avenues extend directly from this study. The most immediate is a systematic 
cross-source evaluation using consumer mobile-phone imagery, for which the 
framework and demonstration pipeline are already in place. Equally important is the 
replication of the pipeline on standard benchmarks (Pittsburgh30k, Tokyo24/7, MSLS) 
to determine which of the observations reported here generalize, and the extension 
of the dataset to include temporal variation (different seasons and capture periods) 
so that long-term robustness can be assessed. Confidence calibration for retrieval — 
a clear weakness highlighted by the rejection analysis — and parameter-efficient 
adaptation techniques such as LoRA represent two further directions with concrete 
potential to improve the practical reliability of the system. Finally, the public release of 
the Kırşehir dataset, or of the scripts required to reconstruct it, would enable 
independent replication and would contribute a non-Western data point to the 
broader visual place recognition landscape. 
Overall, the study supports a measured conclusion: foundation-model-based VPR is 
mature enough to deliver strong single-city localization in under-represented urban 
settings, but the design choices that are considered optimal on large Western 
benchmarks do not automatically transfer. Careful, city-specific empirical validation 
remains essential, and negative results — such as the one reported here for 
LightGlue re-ranking on Kırşehir — are as informative as positive ones for guiding the 
next generation of deployable VPR systems.
